    A generic theory of flagellar oscillations
  • Generalised coordinates: $q = \left(\varphi, A, y\right)\quad\quad$ [in general: $q = \left(\varphi_L, A_L, \varphi_R, A_R, x, y, \alpha\right)$]
  • Balance of active flagellar driving forces $Q$ and friction forces $P = P^{(h)} + P^{(i)} = \Gamma\cdot\dot{q}$
  • Ansatz: $\Gamma^{(i)} = \left(\frac{1}{\eta} - 1\right)\Gamma^{(h)}$
  • No flow ($\dot{y} = u = 0$): $q = \left(\varphi_0, A_0, 0\right)\Rightarrow\dot{q}_0 = \left(\dot{\varphi}_0, 0, 0\right)$
  • Amplitude spring $\kappa_A$ stabilises limit cycle: $Q_{A}\left(\varphi, A\right) = Q_{A}\left(\varphi, A_0\right) - \kappa_A\left(\varphi\right)\left(A - A_0\right)$
    • $\Rightarrow\dot{\varphi}_0\left[\begin{array}{lll} \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{\varphi\varphi}\\ \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{A\varphi} \end{array}\right]_{\left(\varphi, A_0\right)} - \eta\left[\begin{array}{lll} \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{\varphi y} u\\ \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{Ay} u + \kappa_A\Delta A \end{array}\right]_{\left(\varphi, A\right)} = \left[\dot{\varphi}, \dot{A}\right]\cdot\left[\begin{array}{lll} \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{\varphi\varphi} & \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{\varphi A}\\ \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{A\varphi} & \Gamma^{\left(h\right)}_{AA} \end{array}\right]_{\left(\varphi, A\right)}$
    The flagellar load response is characterised by
    the amplitude susceptibility $\chi_{A}$ and the phase speed susceptibility $\chi_{\dot{\varphi}}$

    $\frac{A}{A_0}\approx 1 + \chi_{A}\cdot u$ $\frac{\dot{\varphi}}{\dot{\varphi}_0}\approx 1 + \chi_{\dot{\varphi}}\cdot u$
    The flagellar load response
    is highly phase-dependent
  • during the recovery stroke, the flagella are closer to the cell body
  • the recovery stroke slows down
  • the power stroke speeds up

    The frequency response of the flagellar beat
    reveals dynamic beating modes and shows hysteresis
  • low load: almost linear frequency response
  • intermediate load: chiral beating and tremor-like beating
  • high load: stalling
  • on decreasing load: (delayed) recurrence of beating

    Manifold response of cis and trans flagellum
    to increasing external load
  • chiral beating is almost exclusive to the cis flagellum
  • tremor-like beating is less pronounced for the trans flagellum
  • trans flagellum stalls at lower flow speeds than cis flagellum
    		•
    Delayed response of cis and trans flagellum
    to decreasing external load
  • chiral beating is almost exclusive to the cis flagellum
  • tremor-like beating is less pronounced for the trans flagellum
  • trans flagellum takes longer to resume from stalling
  • some flagella continue stalling at no flow (but resume later)
